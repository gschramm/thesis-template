\section{Inline and displayed equations}

Inline mathematics is enclosed in dollar signs, e.g.\ the energy
$E = mc^{2}$ or the Euler identity $e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$.
For important standalone results, use a numbered displayed equation:

\begin{equation}
  (f * g)(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(\tau)\, g(t - \tau)\, d\tau.
  \label{eq:ch1-convolution}
\end{equation}

The convolution in~\eqref{eq:ch1-convolution} appears throughout
signal processing and probability theory.
Cross-reference equations with \verb|\eqref|, which automatically adds
parentheses.

\section{Aligned multi-line equations}

Use the \texttt{align} environment when a derivation spans several
lines and you want the equals signs to line up.
Each line receives its own label; suppress unwanted labels with
\verb|\notag|.

\begin{align}
  \hat{f}(\xi) &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(t)\,
                   e^{-2\pi i \xi t}\, dt,
                   \label{eq:ch1-fourier-fwd} \\
  f(t)         &= \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi)\,
                   e^{+2\pi i \xi t}\, d\xi.
                   \label{eq:ch1-fourier-inv}
\end{align}

Equations~\eqref{eq:ch1-fourier-fwd}–\eqref{eq:ch1-fourier-inv}
form the Fourier transform pair~\cite{shannon1948}.
A short derivation that needs only one label uses \verb|\notag| on
intermediate lines:

\begin{align}
  \|f + g\|^{2}
    &= \langle f + g,\, f + g \rangle \notag \\
    &= \|f\|^{2} + 2\operatorname{Re}\langle f, g \rangle
       + \|g\|^{2} \notag \\
    &\leq \bigl(\|f\| + \|g\|\bigr)^{2}.
       \label{eq:ch1-triangle}
\end{align}

Inequality~\eqref{eq:ch1-triangle} is the triangle inequality in an
inner-product space.

\section{Piecewise functions and matrix notation}

A piecewise definition uses the \texttt{cases} environment:

\begin{equation}
  \operatorname{ReLU}(x) =
  \begin{cases}
    x & \text{if } x > 0, \\
    0 & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:ch1-relu}
\end{equation}

A fully connected layer applies the linear map in~\eqref{eq:ch1-linear}
followed by a non-linearity such as~\eqref{eq:ch1-relu}:

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{W}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b},
  \qquad
  \mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n},\;
  \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{n},\;
  \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^{m}.
  \label{eq:ch1-linear}
\end{equation}

\section{Typography rules for scientific writing}

Good typography is invisible — it lets the reader focus on the
content rather than the presentation.
The rules below address the most common errors in first drafts.

\paragraph{Dashes.}
Three dash lengths exist.
A \emph{hyphen} (\texttt{-}) joins compound words:
signal-to-noise, well-known.
An \emph{en-dash} (\texttt{-{}-}) denotes a range or connects
equal-weight terms: pages~12--15, the Nyquist--Shannon theorem,
the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality.
An \emph{em-dash} (\texttt{-{}-{}-}) marks a strong parenthetical
break---like this one---with no surrounding spaces.

\paragraph{Non-breaking spaces.}
Use a tilde (\verb|~|) to prevent a line break between a label and
its number: \verb|Figure~\ref{...}|, \verb|Table~\ref{...}|,
\verb|Section~\ref{...}|, \verb|p.~12|.
Without it, the number may be stranded alone at the start of a new
line.

\paragraph{Ellipsis.}
Use \verb|\ldots| or \verb|\dots| to produce a proper ellipsis
($\ldots$), not three typed dots (\verb|...|), which produce
different spacing.
Inside mathematics, \verb|\cdots| ($\cdots$) gives centred dots
for sequences: $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$.

\paragraph{Quotation marks.}
Never use typewriter double-quotes (\verb|"text"|).
In plain \LaTeX{} write \verb|``text''|\ to get ``text''.
With the \texttt{csquotes} package, \verb|\enquote{text}| adapts
automatically to the document language and nesting level:
\enquote{outer \enquote{inner} quote}.

\paragraph{Mathematics in running text.}
Every symbol must be typeset in math mode, even single letters.
Write \emph{the parameter~$\alpha$}, not \emph{the parameter
$\alpha$} without the tilde, and never \emph{the parameter alpha}.
Avoid starting a sentence with a bare symbol; instead of
\emph{$\alpha$ controls the decay}, write
\emph{The parameter~$\alpha$ controls the decay}.

\paragraph{Units.}
Always place a thin space between a number and its unit, and never
italicise units.
The \texttt{siunitx} package handles this correctly and consistently:
\verb|\SI{9.81}{\metre\per\second\squared}| produces
\SI{9.81}{\metre\per\second\squared};
\verb|\SIrange{10}{20}{\kilo\hertz}| produces
\SIrange{10}{20}{\kilo\hertz}.

\paragraph{Decimal alignment in tables.}
Numeric columns look cleaner when digits align on the decimal point.
Use \texttt{siunitx}'s \texttt{S} column type instead of \texttt{c}
to achieve this automatically, without manual padding.

\paragraph{Widows and orphans.}
A \emph{widow} is a single line of a paragraph left at the top of a
page; an \emph{orphan} is a single line left at the bottom.
This template sets \verb|\widowpenalty| and \verb|\clubpenalty| to
\num{10000} in \texttt{preamble.tex}, which effectively forbids both.
